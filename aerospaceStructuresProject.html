// 01 — Overview

Project Overview

This project tasked our team with designing the primary structural member for PRK Aerospace's GTO communications satellite. The goal was to minimize total launch cost — driven by material mass — while ensuring the beam could withstand the specified inertial loading conditions experienced during orbital maneuvers.

We evaluated T, C-channel, and I-beam cross-section geometries across two candidate materials (6061-T6 Aluminum at $2.75/kg and Ti-6Al-4V Titanium at $23.25/kg), using SkyCiv for primary structural analysis. I independently validated the winning design using SolidWorks Simulation.

// 02 — Requirements & Loading

Requirements & Loading Conditions

  • Materials considered: 6061-T6 Aluminum ($2.75/kg) and Ti-6Al-4V Titanium ($23.25/kg)
  • Geometry constraints: Web and flange dimensions ≤ 700 mm; wall thickness ≥ 7 mm
  • Objective: Minimize total launch cost while meeting all structural performance requirements
  • Span: 10 m simply supported beam
  • Applied loads: 27 kN/m distributed over 0–3 m, 18 kN point load at 6 m, 17 kN/m distributed over 9–10 m
  • Reactions: 67.125 kN at x = 0 m, 48.875 kN at x = 8 m
Fig. 2 — Free body diagram with all applied loads and support reactions (SkyCiv).
// 03 — Structural Analysis

Shear & Moment Diagrams

SkyCiv was used to generate shear force and bending moment diagrams across the full 10 m span. The bending moment peaks at approximately 78 kN·m between x = 2–3 m, and a secondary local maximum of −22 kN·m occurs near x = 8 m at the right support. These values governed the required section modulus for the cross-section design.

// 04 — Cross-Section Comparison

Candidate Cross Sections

Three cross-section geometries were evaluated under the thin-walled beam assumption, comparing second moment of area, torsional rigidity, shear flow, warping susceptibility, and total material cost for both materials. All candidate sections used 7 mm wall thickness.

Eliminated

T Cross-Section

  • Required considerably more material to achieve the same structural performance as the other geometries
  • No warping or twisting under symmetric loading — load applied through its axis of symmetry
  • Highest material cost of all three candidates for both aluminum and titanium
  • Most expensive option: $763,863 (Al) / $759,750 (Ti)
Runner-Up

C Cross-Section

  • Most cost-effective option across both materials — $556,145 (Al) / $614,713 (Ti)
  • Largest internal volume: 1% more than I-beam, enabling more instrument storage
  • Susceptible to warping and twisting under the given loading conditions — shear center offset from centroid requires additional analysis or design modifications
  • Only ~$400 cheaper than the I-beam in aluminum — not worth the torsional risk
Selected ✓

I Cross-Section

  • Doubly-symmetric geometry — no warping or twisting under the given loading since load is applied through an axis of symmetry
  • Ranked second most cost-effective: $556,518 (Al) — only ~$400 more than the C-beam
  • Comparable instrument storage volume to the C-beam
  • Superior structural stability and reliability over the mission lifetime

// 05 — Final Design

Final Design Summary

The selected design is a 6061-T6 Aluminum I-beam with 222 mm flanges, 650 mm web height, and 7 mm uniform wall thickness throughout. This geometry satisfies all structural requirements at the lowest viable total launch cost.

The true objective of this project was not simply minimizing material cost, but minimizing total launch cost — which includes both the cost of the material itself and the cost of getting that mass to orbit. With a launch cost of $2,700/kg, every kilogram of structural mass carries a significant premium. The 6061-T6 Aluminum I-beam has a cross-sectional area of approximately 7,658 mm² and a total mass of roughly 206.8 kg over the 10 m span (using Al density of 2,700 kg/m³), contributing $558,360 in launch mass cost alone.

This is precisely why the material selection decision carries so much weight in aerospace design. Titanium (Ti-6Al-4V), despite having a higher specific strength, costs $23.25/kg — roughly 8.5× more per kilogram of material. Even though titanium's higher strength-to-weight ratio could yield a lighter beam, the steep material premium far outweighs the mass savings at this scale, making aluminum the decisive choice for total cost minimization.

// 06 — SolidWorks Validation

FEA Validation

To independently verify the SkyCiv results, I modeled the final I-beam in SolidWorks and ran a Simulation study using the same loading and boundary conditions. Pin supports were applied at x = 0 and x = 8 m; distributed and point loads were applied to match the mission loading profile.

The displacement results from SolidWorks were consistent with the SkyCiv analytical outputs, confirming the beam geometry is correctly sized. The FEA displacement plot shows a minimum of −13.66 mm and a maximum of +6.21 mm, with the largest deflection occurring in the heavily loaded left span.

// 07 — Engineering Thought Process

Walking Through the Design Decisions

01 — Understanding the Problem

The first challenge was learning a new tool, SkyCiv, which turned out to be fairly intuitive, and figuring out how concepts from the course applied to a real design problem. Beyond the mechanics, I was curious about something that isn't immediately obvious: which material would actually produce a lighter beam between aluminum and titanium? Titanium's superior specific strength means less material is needed to carry the same load, so the answer isn't as straightforward as just comparing densities. I was also struck by how dominant launch costs are relative to material costs. At $2,700/kg to orbit, the cost of getting the beam there nearly equals the cost of the beam itself, which fundamentally changes how you think about material selection.

02 — First Instincts

I instinctively ruled out the T-section due to it having fewer structural members than the other two, which turned out to be a fair assumption. I initially thought the C-channel would be the best geometry because of its shape and larger interior cavity compared to the I-beam, useful for housing satellite mechanisms.

03 — The Torsion Problem with the C-Channel

Upon running the numbers, I found the C-channel and I-beam were roughly equivalent in bending strength. However, when you consider the shear center of each section, the I-beam becomes the clear winner. Because the C-channel's shear center lies outside the cross-section and not in line with the applied load, the beam will experience torsion under loading. The I-beam, being doubly symmetric, has its shear center coincident with its centroid and the line of action of the load, so no torsion is induced.

04 — Material Selection Reasoning

For material selection, the decision was fairly straightforward. Aluminum 6061-T6, while less strong than titanium, is considerably cheaper and lighter. Although titanium has superior specific strength, its much higher density means the resulting beam would be comparable in mass or heavier while costing roughly 8.5× more per kilogram in material alone. Since the primary goal of this project is to minimize total launch cost, aluminum is the clear winner. That said, for more demanding applications, material behavior over time should be considered. In a real orbital environment, the satellite experiences extreme temperature swings on every pass, from intense solar exposure to the cold of Earth's shadow, and over thousands of cycles, this thermal cycling induces fatigue in structural members. In that context, titanium's superior resistance to thermal cycling fatigue and its lower coefficient of thermal expansion would make it a more reliable long-term choice, even if it costs more upfront.

05 — Validating with SolidWorks & Next Steps

I chose to run a finite element analysis on the final design because it represents the natural next step in a rigorous engineering process. SkyCiv provides a strong analytical foundation, but SolidWorks Simulation allows for more in-depth validation, including exploring how dynamic loading conditions affect the beam, what the loads actually look like in a real orbital environment, and whether fastener geometry introduces stress concentrations that could compromise the design. Future work would also include optimizing the cross-section geometry to meet an industry-standard factor of safety, and a full fatigue analysis given the cyclic loading environment of an orbiting satellite.

// 08 — Takeaways

Key Takeaways

  • The I-beam's doubly-symmetric geometry eliminated warping and torsion concerns that made the C-channel a structural risk at only ~$400 more in cost, making it clearly the right call.
  • Total launch cost, not just material cost, is the true design driver in aerospace. At $2,700/kg to orbit, the 206.8 kg aluminum beam adds $558,360 in launch mass cost on top of $556,518 in material cost, bringing the real total to ~$1.11M. Titanium's higher strength-to-weight ratio could reduce mass, but its 8.5× higher material price more than cancels out any savings at this scale.
  • SkyCiv and SolidWorks Simulation produced consistent displacement results, validating the analytical thin-walled beam approach as sufficient for preliminary design at this scale.
  • Practiced translating mission-level requirements (minimize launch cost) into quantifiable engineering checks: shear flow, bending stress, deflection, and cross-section geometry.
