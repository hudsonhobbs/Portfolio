Automating a Manual
Coffee Grinder

A personal project applying controls and automation concepts to a real mechanical system — designing and building a motorized drive system for a manual hand grinder.

// 01 — Problem Statement

The Problem

The premise of this course is to take a manual product and design and integrate a system that will automate it, working in a team of four members. My team decided upon automating a manual coffee grinder. The design and customer requirements for the system are as follows.

01
Grind Quality Control
Grind coffee beans to a user-desired level of fineness, preserving grind consistency across uses.
02
Dual Control Modes
Allow the user to adjust grind settings both manually and wirelessly, giving full flexibility over how the system is operated.
03
Scheduled Grinding
Schedule grind times through a digital user interface, enabling automated operation without direct user input.
04
Usage Tracking
Store and report overall usage time to support maintenance awareness and long-term reliability.
Thought Process — Why This Project?

Most of my group members did not feel strongly about which project to pick from the six choices, so I advocated for the coffee grinder because I enjoy drinking coffee and could see myself as a potential customer for the product. Having a personal stake in the outcome is an advantage in design — customer concerns become directly tied to the design process, which tends to produce more thoughtful and user-centered decisions.

The manual coffee grinder selected for automation — featuring a numbered grind setting dial and hand crank.
Thought Process — Design Approach

We broke the system into two subsystems, each containing two subsections that address a distinct mechanical or software challenge.

Subsystem 1 — Manual Control
Subsection 1.1
Process to Change Grind Setting

Because we need high torque and a small range of motion, the natural choice for the team was a 0–180 degree servo motor. The follow-on engineering problem then became: how do we transmit the rotation from the servo to the grinder's adjustment dial?

Subsection 1.2
Process to Turn the Grinder Burr

The immediate candidates were a DC motor or a stepper motor. An additional design requirement the team established was stall detection — and the DC motor is the right choice to meet this. By monitoring current draw with a current sensing module, we can detect a stall without the step-skipping problems that would affect a stepper motor under load. As a bonus, since motor speed is controllable, we can track exactly how long the motor and burrs have been running, directly supporting the usage tracking requirement.

Subsystem 2 — Wireless Control
Subsection 2.1
Wireless Control of Product / App

With only four months to develop this project, instead of building a market-ready app, the team prioritized a high-quality prototype — something easily replicated and usable on any user's phone, capable of reliably controlling the grinder within the user's home. We chose a WiFi HTTP approach to demonstrate full functionality. For future development, we recommend transitioning to a proper iOS/Android app using MQTT, which would enable control from anywhere and represent a significantly more finished product.

// 02 — Subassembly 1

Manual Functionality

This subassembly covers all the physical functions of the device: the DC motor drive, grind time control, fineness setting switching, and noise output. It is the mechanical backbone of the system.

Construction & Assembly

How It's Built

Bottom Frame

A gray 3D-printed frame with an integrated gasket holds the grinder body by friction fit, preventing the bottom half from rotating during operation. Screw holes on the back of the frame secure a bracketed servo motor in place. A 3D-printed fan belt attachment fits onto the servo motor coupling, and a rubber belt — glued and taped to a circular screw clamp — connects the two. Holes cut into the belt mesh with teeth on the fan belt attachment, allowing the servo to step the grind setting up or down through each fineness position.

Top Frame

The top frame is a separate 3D-printed part that friction fits over the top of the coffee grinder. It features holes that align with the motor's screw holes, allowing it to be bolted securely in place. A custom 3D-printed shaft coupling is pressed onto the motor shaft. To install, push the top frame down until resistance is felt, then twist and lightly press down to slot the grinder's shaft into the coupling.

Functionality

How It Works

Once assembled, the system handles two independent physical functions. The DC motor drives the grinder burr through the shaft coupling, controlling grind time and duration. The servo motor, connected via the belt and fan belt attachment, rotates the grind setting collar up or down to the desired fineness level. Together these two actuators replicate the full manual operation of the grinder without any user input.

Known Problems

What Needs Improvement

Fan Belt Durability

The fan belt solution is not permanent. Under extended use, the belt slips from the tape and stops functioning as intended. A more robust mechanical connection — such as a printed belt with integrated teeth or a gear-driven alternative — would be needed for a production version.

Servo Coupling Strength

The 3D-printed piece connecting the servo to the fan belt does not have sufficient strength for long-term use. It failed after the demo video was recorded. This part would need to be redesigned with a stronger geometry, printed in a more durable material, or replaced with a machined component.

Frame Bowing

When the fan belt is attached and under tension, it causes the back of the frame to bow inwards considerably. While this does not prevent the system from functioning, it is not acceptable for a consumer product and points to a need for a stiffer frame design or a revised belt tensioning approach.

// 03 — Subassembly 2

Wireless Functionality

This subassembly handles all wireless communication and user interface features, allowing the grinder to be controlled remotely from any phone on the same network.

Platform & Hardware

How It's Built

Controller — ESP32

The team selected the ESP32 microcontroller as the wireless hub due to its built-in WiFi capability and seamless integration with the Blynk platform. The ESP32 handles all communication between the Blynk app and the physical actuators from Subassembly 1.

App — Blynk

Blynk is a drag-and-drop app development tool that allows rapid prototyping of mobile interfaces without writing native iOS or Android code. The team used AI to write the ESP32 firmware using the Blynk library, significantly accelerating development within the four-month project window. The resulting app is easily replicated on any user's phone and reliably controls the grinder over a home WiFi network.

Functionality

Built-In Features

01
Start / Stop Grinding
Remotely start and stop the DC motor drive from anywhere on the home network.
02
Fineness Selection
Select any grind fineness setting wirelessly, triggering the servo to move to the correct position.
03
Usage Time Tracking
Tracks cumulative motor run time and displays it in the app to inform the user when maintenance is due.
04
Maintenance Reset
After servicing the grinder, the user can reset the maintenance counter directly from the app.
Video 2 — Wireless functionality demonstration via the Blynk app on ESP32.
Known Problems

What Needs Improvement

App Reliability

The app did not always behave consistently, particularly when reconnecting to a network after a dropout. This is a known limitation of the WiFi HTTP approach and would likely be resolved by transitioning to MQTT, which handles reconnection and message delivery more robustly.

App Distribution

Currently there is no clear path to making the app available to end users outside of the development environment. Packaging and distributing a Blynk-based app to consumers is a challenge that would need to be solved before this could reach a wider audience. The recommended long-term solution is to develop a proper iOS/Android app using MQTT, which would be distributable through standard app stores.
